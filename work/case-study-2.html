Audit Management Tool

Redesigning the daily workspace three tax consultants lived inside — without making them relearn it.

Three consultants were uploading the same file three times. Nobody had told them not to — the system just never said anything had happened. That was the first thing I noticed, and it told me what kind of project this was going to be.

The client asked my team to redesign the platform, the tool their tax consultants spent most of their working day inside. The brief asked for two things: clean up the friction in the existing screens, and fold new features in without making anyone re-learn the product.

I had never touched financial compliance before. So before I drew anything, I gave myself one rule: shut up and watch.

Observations

What I noticed before talking to anyone

Before any interviews, I spent time inside the existing Compliance Platform interface — clicking through every screen as a first-time user. These are the problems that were visible immediately, without needing domain knowledge.

01 / 04

The sidebar was taking up space the data needed

A fixed vertical sidebar sat on the left of every screen. The platform is built around wide tables and long forms — and the sidebar was eating into that space on every single one. It was the first thing I noticed.

02 / 04

Uploading a file gave no indication it had worked

After dropping a file into the upload area, nothing changed on screen. No count, no confirmation, no visible state. I immediately understood why consultants were uploading files multiple times — there was simply no way to know if the first one had gone through.

03 / 04

The tables were hard to read across

Every row in the compliance tables was the same white background with no visual separation. Tracking a single row of data across a wide screen required conscious effort each time. On a task consultants repeat dozens of times a day, that adds up quickly.

04 / 04

Colour was doing one job, not the important one

Green was used to show which tab was active — and that was it. A warning notice sitting inside a bright green banner, on an already green screen, didn't read as a warning at all. When everything is the same colour, nothing stands out.

User Research

Talking to the people who actually used it

I spoke directly with three consultants who used Compliance Platform every day. I asked them to walk me through their actual tasks, not just describe them. I found a huge mismatch between how they actually work and how the system was designed to work.

“The tool worked — but it wasn’t working for the consultants using it. There’s a difference, and that difference was the entire project.”

As someone with no background in financial compliance, I could not assume I understood the workflow from the brief alone. The consultants were my primary source of truth. Five clear problems emerged from those conversations.

The Workflow Order Mismatch

The most revealing finding was how the system’s task flow contradicted the consultants’ real-world mental model. Here’s exactly what that difference looked like — and why it mattered.

Operational trigger Primary work Sequential Admin / reference

The consultant has already selected the client by the time a task opens — so Client Details and Permission are reference, not workflow. Document Upload is the real operational trigger; Form 3CD is where 90% of the work happens. Reordering the surface to match that reality removed a cognitive switch from every single task.

Why the tables were tiring to read

Striped rows and taller cells so the eye tracks across a wide row without losing its place — and right-aligned, comma-grouped figures so the numbers line up and totals are scannable. Exactly what a data-heavy audit table needs.

Results

What changed — and what stayed put

Same product. Same buttons. Fewer cuts.

The redesign was structural, not visual. Consultants who’d learned the old system didn’t lose their muscle memory — they just stopped fighting it.

I deliberately didn’t make the new product look unrecognisable. Consultants had already learned where things lived. A dramatic overhaul would have been a second tax on people who deal with enough taxes already. The changes happened underneath.

Tasks completed faster

The flow now matches the real order of work, cutting unnecessary steps on every compliance task.

Branch responsibilities became traceable

A business problem that was invisible in the interface was explicitly modelled in the design.

Duplicate uploads dropped

Consultants could see exactly what had been uploaded and stopped second-guessing the system.

Tables became easier to read

Striped rows and field-framed cells let the eye track across a wide row without losing its place — cutting misreads on the most data-heavy screens.

Screen real estate served the data

With the sidebar removed, the most demanding screens finally had the horizontal space they needed.

Reflection

What this project taught me

Enterprise software is built around processes. But processes are run by people. The job isn’t to make the screens prettier — it’s to close the gap between how the software works and how the human in front of it works.

I knew nothing about tax compliance when I started. That helped. It meant I asked the obvious questions someone closer to the system would have stopped asking years ago. The three consultants I sat with were patient and honest — their answers basically wrote this project.

One question ran through every decision: does this match how a consultant actually thinks? If no, I changed it. If yes, I left it alone. That was the whole project.
